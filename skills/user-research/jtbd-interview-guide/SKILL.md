# JTBD Interview Guide Skill

## Purpose
Build a complete Jobs To Be Done interview package — from screener to discussion guide to synthesis template — so the PM walks into customer conversations with clear structure and walks out with research that can populate an OST opportunity space and inform product strategy.

---

## Background: JTBD Framework

The core JTBD insight: customers don't buy products — they **hire** products to make progress in a specific circumstance. Understanding the "job" means understanding:

- The **situation** that triggered the purchase or adoption (the struggling moment)
- The **progress** the customer was trying to make (functional, emotional, social dimensions)
- The **forces** that drove and blocked the switch to your product
- The **anxieties and tradeoffs** they navigated

### The Four Forces of Progress

```
PUSHING AWAY from old solution        PULLING TOWARD new solution
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Push: Pain/frustration with          Pull: Attraction to new
the current situation                solution's promise

                    ↕                           ↕

Habit: Comfort with current          Anxiety: Fear, uncertainty
situation / inertia                  about the new solution
```

A switch happens when Push + Pull > Habit + Anxiety.

### Two JTBD Interview Types

| Type | When to Use | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Switching Interview** | Customer recently adopted your product or switched from a competitor | Understand the decision journey and job that triggered the switch |
| **Progress Interview** | Existing customer using the product regularly | Understand the ongoing jobs, struggles, and workarounds in current usage |

## Step 1: Gather Context

Ask in a single batch if not provided:

- **What is the product or feature area?** (grounds the interview scope)
- **What is the research question?** (what specific thing are you trying to learn?)
- **Who are the target interviewees?** (customer segment, persona, lifecycle stage)
- **Interview type:** Switching interview, progress interview, or both?
- **How many interviews are planned?**
- **What do you already believe is true?** (current hypotheses to test)
- **What decisions will this research inform?** (OST opportunity space, roadmap, positioning, pricing)
- **Interview format:** Live video call, in-person, async written?

---

## Step 2: Screener Criteria

Define who qualifies for the interview. A bad screener fills your calendar with the wrong people.

### Screener Template

**Qualifying criteria (must meet ALL):**
- [ ] [Customer segment or role — e.g., "Operations manager at a company with 50-500 employees"]
- [ ] [Lifecycle stage — e.g., "Has been using the product for at least 30 days"]
- [ ] [Relevant behavior — e.g., "Has used the [specific feature area] at least once"]
- [ ] [Recency — e.g., "Signed up or made a key decision within the last 6 months" (for switching interviews)]

**Disqualifying criteria (exclude if ANY apply):**
- [ ] [Competitor employees, investors, or personal connections]
- [ ] [Power users or internal advocates — they're atypical; interview separately]
- [ ] [Customers still in onboarding or trial — not enough context yet]

**Ideal mix:**
- For switching interviews: Seek variety in prior solutions (what they switched from)
- For progress interviews: Include a mix of high-engagement and moderate-engagement users
- Aim for 6-8 interviews to reach saturation for a focused research question

## Step 3: Discussion Guide

### Pre-Interview Setup

**Before the call:**
- Review the customer's account data (signup date, features used, plan tier)
- Pull any support tickets or NPS verbatims associated with this customer
- Prepare to take verbatim notes — exact language matters in JTBD research

**Opening (2 min):**
Say: *"I want to learn about your experience — specifically, what was going on in your work or life that led you to [start using this product / start using this feature]. I'm not here to pitch or sell anything. I want to understand your situation from your point of view. There are no wrong answers — push back on my questions if they don't fit your experience."*

---

### Discussion Guide: Switching Interview

*(Use when the customer recently adopted the product or switched from something else)*

---

**Part 1: The First Thought (5-10 min)**

Goal: Find the struggling moment — the first time they knew the old way wasn't working.

- "Take me back to when you first started thinking about finding a different solution. What was going on?"
- "What were you using before? What made that work for you?"
- "Was there a specific moment — a day, a situation — when you thought 'I need something different'?"
- "What were you trying to accomplish that wasn't working?"

*Listen for: Specific triggering events, not generic dissatisfaction. Push for concrete situations.*

---

**Part 2: The Search (5-10 min)**

Goal: Understand what alternatives were considered and what criteria mattered.

- "When you decided to look for something new, what did you do first?"
- "How did you find out about [product]?"
- "What else did you look at or try?"
- "What made you decide to give [product] a shot?"
- "Was there anyone else involved in the decision?"

*Listen for: The evaluation criteria they actually used, not the ones they think they should have used.*

---

**Part 3: The Decision (5-10 min)**

Goal: Understand the moment of commitment and what pushed them over the line.

- "Walk me through the decision to actually sign up / purchase / move forward."
- "What were you most excited about?"
- "What were you most worried about?"
- "Was there anything that almost made you not do it?"
- "What finally made you say 'yes'?"

*Listen for: The pull (promise of the new solution) and the anxiety (fear of making a mistake).*

---

**Part 4: First Use & Progress (5-10 min)**

Goal: Understand whether the product delivered on the job.

- "Walk me through the first time you used it. What did you do?"
- "Did it work the way you expected?"
- "What surprised you — good or bad?"
- "At what point did you feel like it was actually working for you?"
- "Is there anything you still do the old way, even though you have [product]?"

*Listen for: The "first win" moment, residual workarounds, and unmet expectations.*

---

**Part 5: The Job (10 min)**

Goal: Articulate the actual job — the progress the customer was trying to make.

- "When you think about what [product] does for you — what's the main thing it helps you do?"
- "If [product] disappeared tomorrow, what would you do instead?"
- "How would you describe [product] to a colleague who'd never heard of it?"
- "What's the most important thing it does for you?"

*Listen for: Their language, not your language. The job is expressed in their words.*

---

### Discussion Guide: Progress Interview

*(Use with existing customers to map ongoing jobs, struggles, and workarounds)*

---

**Part 1: Role & Context (5 min)**

- "Tell me about your role and what a typical week looks like for you."
- "How does [product] fit into that?"
- "How often do you use it and for what?"

---

**Part 2: The Job in Action (10-15 min)**

- "Walk me through the last time you used [product / feature]. Start from what you were trying to do."
- "What triggered you to open it up that day?"
- "What happened step by step?"
- "Where did things go smoothly?"
- "Where did you get stuck or have to figure something out?"
- "Did you have to use anything else to finish the job? (other tools, manual steps, workarounds)"

*Listen for: Workarounds are gold — they reveal unmet jobs and opportunity space.*

---

**Part 3: Friction & Struggle (10 min)**

- "What's the most frustrating part of doing [this task] right now?"
- "Is there anything you wish [product] did that it doesn't?"
- "What takes longer than it should?"
- "Have you ever given up partway through and done something differently?"
- "What do you do when [product] doesn't quite do what you need?"

---

**Part 4: Value & Stakes (5-10 min)**

- "What would happen if you couldn't use [product] for a week?"
- "What's the most important outcome you get from it?"
- "If you could change one thing, what would it be?"

## Step 4: JTBD Statement Synthesis

After interviews, synthesize findings into structured JTBD statements using the standard format.

### JTBD Statement Format

```
When [situation / trigger],
I want to [motivation / progress being sought],
So I can [expected outcome / job to be done].
```

**Add dimensions:**
- **Functional job:** The practical task being accomplished
- **Emotional job:** How they want to feel (or avoid feeling)
- **Social job:** How they want to be perceived by others

**Example:**
> When our ops team is preparing for a client review,  
> I want to pull together usage data without involving the engineering team,  
> So I can show clients ROI quickly and look on top of things in the meeting.
>
> - Functional job: Self-serve reporting
> - Emotional job: Feel confident and prepared
> - Social job: Appear competent and proactive to clients

## Step 6: Quality Checklist

- [ ] Screener excludes power users and atypical segments for the main round
- [ ] Discussion guide opens with the first thought / struggling moment — not product feedback
- [ ] Questions are open-ended (no leading questions, no yes/no questions)
- [ ] JTBD statements use customer language, not product/feature language
- [ ] Synthesis distinguishes frequency (heard by many) from severity (deeply felt by few)
- [ ] Workarounds are captured as opportunity inputs
- [ ] Forces analysis covers all four dimensions (push/pull/habit/anxiety)

---

## Output Format

- Default to producing the full package: screener + discussion guide + synthesis template
- Produce the switching interview guide unless the user specifies progress interviews
- Tailor the discussion guide to the specific product area and research question provided
- Flag where the user should customize based on their specific product (marked with `[customize]`)
- Offer to produce JTBD statements from raw interview notes if the user pastes them in
- Pair with the opportunity-solution-tree skill to map interview findings directly into an OST
